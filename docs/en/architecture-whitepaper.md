## Preface

What I am doing is handing my own judgment and domain knowledge, as a whole package, over to an AI — training the AI to become a version of me that never tires.

This white paper describes an AI-collaborative research workflow that I have been building and continuously iterating on for more than half a year: a three-layer collaboration architecture (rules layer, thinking layer, execution layer) plus a folder-governance foundation, organizing the entire process of generative AI participation in academic research, with the emphasis on controllable and reproducible quality. Once the foundation is properly laid, every subsequent effort can go into building the tower upward instead of clearing rubble.

I believe the solution does not lie at the level of the single conversation (prompt), but in building the researcher's judgment into a complete architecture that AI can read, execute, and audit (harnessing).

## 1. The Problem: After AI Enters Research Work

Generative AI has genuinely entered the daily routine of academic research — literature summaries, draft polishing, data tidying; most researchers have used it to some degree. But most usage is piecemeal: open a chat window, paste in a passage, take back an output. This "loose-parts usage" works well for low-risk tasks, but the moment it reaches the core links of research production, two classes of risk surface immediately.

The first class is quality risk. Large language models fabricate citations to literature that does not exist, attach forged page numbers and figures to real publications, and pair real authors with positions they never held. More insidiously, fabrication is not confined to sentences carrying a citation. Seemingly neutral declarative sentences often conceal "facts" the model has filled in from its training memory, along with causal claims that exceed what the research design can support. Moreover, models degrade over long processes: quality slides in the later portions of batch analyses; by the second half of a process the model forgets the rules laid down in the first; a strong professional judgment gets retracted because the user insisted. These are not transient defects of some model version but structural weaknesses of the technology at its current stage.

The second class is integrity risk. When AI participates deeply in literature analysis and writing, how does the researcher ensure that citations are genuine and traceable? In qualitative research, if AI verifies coded excerpts "from impression," it is effectively manufacturing a qualitative deepfake: the words inside the quotation marks appear to come from the interviewee, but have in fact been seamlessly rewritten by the model. The more fundamental problem is researcher agency: let AI pose the research questions, set the analytical framework, and write the argumentative spine, and the researcher gradually slides from author to signatory. Once that line is breached, even the strictest citation checking is merely standing guard over a paper that is no longer one's own.

The common responses to these two classes of risk are two extremes: prohibition, or reliance on user self-discipline and prompting skill. I take a third path — one that comes from the disciplinary perspective of public administration, and from my practical observation inside the public sector: how to make a highly capable but not fully reliable agent do the right thing is a question neither this discipline nor this field finds unfamiliar, and the answer has never been to prohibit delegation, nor to hope the agent disciplines itself, but institutions. Through division of labor, delegation of authority, auditing, version management, and the allocation of responsibility, treat the AI as an extraordinarily capable, tireless, yet error-prone research assistant in need of supervision — and the researcher (the user of the AI tool), as the supervising professor, governs it by designing institutions.

Across the entire workflow, the single most critical line of defense is the citation-authenticity audit that runs through the whole production chain, so that every claim can be traced to its owner and every quotation traced back to its source. The sections below explain, step by step, the design principles of this architecture, the hand-off discipline among the seven stages, the maintenance and governance mechanisms, and the hard-won lessons accumulated during development, for researchers who intend to adopt or adapt it.

## 2. The Three-Layer Architecture: Rules Layer, Thinking Layer, Execution Layer

The whole workflow consists of three layers. What this layering answers is not "where do the files go?" but "what do the human and the AI each externalize for the other?": **the rules layer is written for the AI (who I am, how to collaborate with me); the thinking layer is written for oneself (what this paper is actually meant to argue — the AI merely reads it in passing); the execution layer is where human and machine work together (the seven stages)**. Beneath the three layers sits a governance base: the folder read/write rules, which determine where outputs go and who may write where. Mapped onto the starter kit's file layout: the rules layer resides in the four control files in `00_專案控制/` together with the project rules of each folder; the carrier of the thinking layer is `paper-structure.md`; the execution layer is the seven stage skills; and the governance base is the read/write zoning of folders 00 through 05.

### 2.1 The Rules Layer: Externalizing Judgment

The question the rules layer answers is: on what basis does the AI know how to collaborate with *you*? Its substance is the four control files placed in `00_專案控制/`.

For most people, the knowledge of collaborating with AI lives in conversational memory: the preferences explained clearly this time must be explained all over again in the next new conversation, and many more judgments have never been articulated at all, existing only in the researcher's own intuition. The design idea behind the control files is to write these repeatedly used judgments into "institutions the AI can read" — what I call the **externalization of judgment**: when something truly counts as done, when to stop and ask a human, which signals mean the direction is wrong, how to verify the quality floor — all written out as explicit criteria accompanied by positive and negative examples. For instance, the criterion for "done" requires that the acceptance conditions be checked off item by item, the output actually land on disk, and no residual errors remain — all three — and states in plain words that "merely satisfying 'the output looks plausible' does not count as done"; the criterion for verification establishes that "self-checking within the same context inherits one's own blind spots and does not count as verification" — important outputs must be handed to a fresh agent carrying no prior context to read back and restate. Once externalized, judgment no longer clings to the memory of any single conversation; it becomes an institutional asset that can be reused across conversations, examined, and continuously revised.

The four control files each have their charge. `about-me.md` records who you are, your goals and schedule pressures, the research hours you can invest each week, your priority rules when multiple tasks compete — or anything else that helps the AI collaborate with you better. Having read it, the AI turns from passive executor into staff officer: when tasks pile up, it actively judges priority by your rules and guards your research time for you. `research-identity.md` records your research positioning and theoretical map, graded by depth of mastery. The AI calibrates its collaboration depth accordingly: for theories you have mastered in depth it no longer lectures from the basics; for methods you have marked "not yet independently operated" it volunteers detailed guidance and pitfall warnings. `writing-standards.md` holds the writing norms and quality thresholds: requirements for argumentative structure, a prohibition list (including "the typical traces of AI writing"), and citation-honesty rules; paragraphs that fall short must be actively flagged by the AI as "this paragraph needs revision, because:" rather than delivered as-is. `portfolio-status.md` is the dynamic dashboard, carrying all time-sensitive information (the progress of each paper, schedule events), maintained separately from the stable facts of the first three files so that time-bound information does not scatter and go stale.

One mechanism worth singling out is the "**self-declaration of writing habits**" in the about-me template: the user honestly itemizes their own known bad habits of writing and thinking. Taking myself as the example, I specifically flagged such genuine vices of mine as "my literature reviews tend to expand without limit" and "my meta-perspective is so strong it tends to escalate endlessly," noting the triggering situations and how I would like the AI to intervene. The seven stages read this section in the course of their work and actively warn or intercept at the very moment the habit surfaces. This amounts to outsourcing "self-awareness" to a collaborator that never tires: you declare your own weaknesses, and the AI stands permanent guard. In my own experience, this is the mechanism with the highest return on investment in the entire system.

The rules layer adopts a nested, outside-in design: the outermost layer is the personal identity and criteria that remain stable across projects (the four control files); the middle layer is general working rules that span tasks; the innermost layer is the contextual rules of a specific project (each folder's README and project instructions). The further out, the more stable; the further in, the closer to the work at hand. When executing a task, the AI reads from the outside in — first getting to know you, then your working habits, and only then entering this particular project. And whatever the layer, one key principle is shared: **write all of it in your own words**. Control files are not forms to fill in; they are a letter written to the AI you regularly use. What it reads is your language, and only then will the way it responds feel like working with you.

### 2.2 The Thinking Layer: Written for Yourself — the AI Only Reads It in Passing

The thinking layer is the most easily misunderstood of the three. Its carrier, `paper-structure.md`, plays the role of hub in the pipeline (see Section 3), but in essence it is not a spec file for the AI; it is **the externalization of the researcher's thinking**: what question I am answering (written clearly in one sentence — a question, not a topic); what my core thesis is (what I want to persuade the reader to believe, and why it holds); my argumentative path (the order of reasoning from question to conclusion, and which steps are the critical ones); what support I will draw on (why these theories and this literature rather than others); and, most important of all, one column for **the sticking points I have not yet thought through — written down honestly**.

This layer is written for you to read; the AI only reads it in passing. This positioning directly answers the agency risk raised in Section 1: the framework of the research is always set down by you first, and all of the AI's downstream work (search, analysis, synthesis, drafting) is anchored to the thinking you wrote down; where you have not yet thought things through, what the institution requires is honest flagging, not letting the AI do the thinking for you. The contrast with the rules layer makes it clearer still: the rules layer externalizes **stable judgment** (criteria and preferences that do not vary across projects), while the thinking layer externalizes **thinking in progress** (questions, theses, and sticking points that evolve as the research advances). Both are externalizations; what is externalized differs, and so does the reader.

### 2.3 The Execution Layer: Seven Stages

The execution layer houses "repetitive work that must stay stable": seven stage skills decompose "AI-assisted research" into seven stages with clearly bounded responsibilities: research design (research-design-diagnosis), literature search (literature-search), per-paper analysis (literature-analysis), cross-paper synthesis (literature-synthesis), chapter drafting (chapter-drafting), review diagnosis (review-diagnosis), and qualitative thematic analysis (thematic-analysis). You can think of them as a fully staffed research team: a literature-search assistant, a literature-summary assistant, a literature-synthesis assistant, two co-authors (one to develop the framework with you, one to draft with you paragraph by paragraph), plus an independent external review consultant. The difference is that this team never tires, can be audited, and every member's authority is explicitly limited by institution.

Decomposing the work into multiple stages is not for the sake of formal tidiness; it is the precondition of quality control. A single "omnipotent research assistant" cannot be audited: its every step is blended into the same conversation, and when an error occurs there is no way to localize it and no way to redo it. Once cut into stages, each stage has an explicit input contract, output format, and quality gate; upstream and downstream hand off through files; errors are confined within a single stage; and both auditing and redoing gain a point of purchase. This is less the modularization of software engineering than the oldest wisdom of bureaucracy: offices divided and posts assigned, each with its own charge.

### 2.4 The Governance Base: Folder Read/Write Rules

The question the governance base answers is: where do the AI's outputs go, and who may write where? The workspace maps six folders onto the phases of the research process: `00_專案控制/`, `01_文獻/`, `02_資料與證據/`, `03_寫作/`, `04_審稿與回應/`, `05_輸出/`. The governance rule has only one main line: **the five folders 00 through 04 are read-only during task execution — the AI reads but never writes; `05_輸出/` is the sole write zone for AI output**.

This seemingly simple rule carries two responsibilities. First, protecting the originals: your literature library, raw data, and finalized chapters can never be overwritten by any single slip of the AI. Irreversible damage is ruled out at the architectural level, rather than depending on the AI being careful every single time. Second, establishing the rhythm of "produce, review, promote": all AI outputs land first in `05_輸出/`, and only after your review do you manually promote them to the official zones (finalized analyses are promoted into the literature library; finalized chapters move into `03_寫作/`). The AI is responsible for production; the human is responsible for signing off — and the act of signing off is precisely moving the file across that read-only line. The division of responsibility is therefore not a soft declaration that counts merely because it appears in the documentation, but a hard constraint enforced by the workflow institution and the provisions of each stage, with "manual file-moving = sign-off" as the final line of defense.

Here an honest declaration must be made: this read-only line is enforced by each stage's skill instructions, not by hard permissions at the operating-system level. The starter kit does not — and cannot — set file permissions for you; in an environment where the AI has write access to the workspace, it can technically write to any folder, and what stops it is institution and provision, not permission bits. The truly irreversible protection comes from two human-side arrangements: the version history of the cloud-synced folder (mistaken writes can be restored), and the promotion discipline that "output must first land in `05_輸出/`, and only a human manually moving the file makes it count." If you need harder protection, you may additionally set read-only attributes on folders `01` through `04`.

## 3. The Seven Stages and Their Hand-off Discipline

### 3.1 One Pipeline, One Hub

The seven stages are not seven independent tools but one pipeline with an explicit data flow, and the hub is precisely the thinking layer's carrier, `paper-structure.md`. Your externalized thinking is the coordinate system of the entire pipeline:

```
research-design-diagnosis ──produces──▶ paper-structure.md (the paper-structure file, hub of the whole pipeline)
        │                                       │
        ▼ (marks each section                   │ (read / written back
           "ready for downstream")              │  by the stages)
literature-search ──DOI list──▶ literature-analysis ──dual files──▶ literature-synthesis
                                                                          │
thematic-analysis (primary-data branch) ──thematic skeleton──┐            ▼ (synthesis dual files)
                                                             └──▶ chapter-drafting ──draft──▶ review-diagnosis
```

The research-design stage produces paper-structure.md and marks, section by section, which section has matured enough to launch its downstream; literature search reads its research question and theoretical framework to build the literature pool; per-paper analysis, on completion, writes back to its "literature analysis completion list" (the single exception explicitly authorized under the read-only rule); cross-paper synthesis works from that completion list; chapter drafting reads its outline to draft; review diagnosis, in self-review mode, uses it to check the claim strength of the full text. Qualitative thematic analysis is a parallel branch — the only one of the seven that handles primary data (interview transcripts, field notes) — feeding its thematic skeleton directly into chapter drafting. Review diagnosis is the cross-cutting quality-assurance stage, standing outside the linear pipeline and reviewing the final products of all the other branches.

### 3.2 What Each of the Seven Stages Does

**Research design (research-design-diagnosis)** is responsible for structural construction at the chapter, section, and subsection levels, and is the starting point of the entire pipeline. It supports three working directions (forward: from question to method and contribution; reverse: from existing methods and results back to the question; proposal: from gap to design), unfolding the structure level by level through a laddered three-phase process. Its most important design is the **commitment gate**: at the three key decision points — choosing the working direction, the core research question, and the contribution claim — the user must first state their own answer before the AI offers its suggestions, and this order may not be inverted. Look at the AI's answer first and then "choose," and the researcher's framework has already been anchored. After configuration is complete, it also enforces an adversarial check: running counter-arguments against the settled framework, and refusing to withdraw its challenge merely because the user insists.

**Literature search (literature-search)** builds the literature pool. Its core principle is **API-grounded**: all candidate literature may come only from real returns of the OpenAlex and Semantic Scholar academic-database APIs, and generation from the model's training memory is forbidden; even for the seed papers the AI recommends from memory, every DOI must be verified one by one by script, and any mismatch is replaced through a fresh search by title. The process runs "seed round (whitelist and nominations → verification → bidirectional citation-network expansion) → keyword round (collaboratively designed search strings → trial search → formal search) → semantic scoring and bucketing → user adjudication," with all API calls, scoring, and bucketing recorded in an auditable search log. When the candidate pool overflows, it does not truncate silently; it halts and reports the distribution, leaving the user to decide whether to tighten the criteria or accept the cost.

**Per-paper analysis (literature-analysis)** produces a deep analysis for each PDF. Its key architecture is the **separation of knowledge file and application file**: the knowledge file records what the paper itself says (independent of any paper of yours, reusable across papers over the long term), while the application file records what use it has for the paper you are currently writing (bound to a paper ID). When the same piece of literature is used in a different research project, the knowledge deposit need not be redone; only a new application file is added. Each recommendation in the application file must furthermore be tagged by its type of basis (endogenous to the literature, contextual bridging, or analyst inference); inference-type items must be flagged as pending human confirmation and must not be written up as established conclusions of the literature. (Advanced option: pair this set of knowledge and application files with a bidirectional-linking tool such as Obsidian to build a cross-project literature library that automatically surfaces "which papers have cited this same work"; for the complete working logic, from Zotero collection to building the Obsidian vault, see [advanced_obsidian-integration.md](./advanced_obsidian-integration.md).)

**Cross-paper synthesis (literature-synthesis)** integrates multiple analyses into a theoretical knowledge map with explicit literature attribution. Its iron rule is that "**every claim must have an owner**": every argument, definition, and mechanism in the synthesis file is annotated with its source paper and location, and unattributed exposition is forbidden; a consensus claim requires support from at least two papers; a controversy must have its level marked, and no fake controversies may be manufactured. On launch it forcibly computes the set difference and reports "so many papers analyzed, so many not yet analyzed"; unanalyzed literature must never be silently blended in. Gaps invisible to the downstream must be converted into explicit decisions placed before the user's eyes.

**Chapter drafting (chapter-drafting)** turns the settled structure and the synthesis results into journal prose. It advances at the rhythm of "write one paragraph, confirm one paragraph": for each paragraph it first presents an argumentative skeleton (argument steps, citation candidates, sentence-pattern options) for the user to confirm, then fleshes it out into text; once approved, the paragraph is immediately written to file and marked frozen, and writing continuously across paragraphs is strictly forbidden. Citation follows a strict whitelist: only literature that has passed analysis and synthesis review may be cited; literature outside the whitelist is absolutely barred from citation, and when the need arises the stage pauses and presents the user with options.

**Review diagnosis (review-diagnosis)** is the quality-assurance stage, supporting modes for thesis-defense examination, journal refereeing, pre-submission self-review, and point-by-point response to external reviewers — with the greatest severity applied to one's own manuscript (self-review mode). Its two signature designs: first, the **pre-committed scoring plan** — the scoring plan is output and placed on record before the manuscript is read, and may not be revised afterward, preventing the post hoc rationalization of fitting the scoring logic to the manuscript once seen; second, the **concession threshold** — only a factual error, in-manuscript evidence not previously grasped, or a stronger counter-argument constitute grounds for the AI to withdraw a review comment; the author's emotional insistence, restatement of position, or appeal to authority do not. The latter writes the structural weakness that "the model backs down when persuaded" directly into provision.

**Qualitative thematic analysis (thematic-analysis)** handles primary qualitative data, with a "one skill, two paths" design: before launch it settles whether to take the reflexive TA path or the coding-reliability path, and thereafter intercepts any mixing of the two paradigms throughout (for example, choosing the reflexive path yet demanding inter-coder reliability coefficients be reported); methodological consistency is guarded by the stage on your behalf. Its entrance carries a hard gate: if the data has not passed de-identification verification (including named confirmation that the research-ethics consent form covers AI processing), it refuses to start.

### 3.3 The Core Selling Point: A Citation-Authenticity Defense Line Spanning the Whole Chain

If you remember only one thing about this workflow, it should be this: **anti-fabrication is not the function of any single stage, but six lines of defense installed along the entire production chain**. The reason is simple: hallucinations are inherited. A fabricated literature claim that slips into a knowledge file at the analysis layer will be written into the theoretical map by the synthesis layer, then cited into the paper's text by the drafting layer, acquiring legitimacy layer by layer. Single-point defense necessarily fails; the defense must span the chain.

The first line stands at the entrance: literature search's API-grounded principle and mandatory DOI verification ensure that every paper entering the literature pool actually exists. The second is the **material-first clause**, gated repeatedly at the analysis, synthesis, and drafting layers: specific claims about a work (figures, page numbers, quotations, author positions) may come only from the PDFs actually loaded in this run or from upstream analysis outputs; the model's training memory may serve only to suggest search directions, and any content for which no basis can be found must be labeled "memory, unverified" and may not enter formal output. The third is the analysis layer's dual-file separation and inference audit, preventing "what the literature says" and "what the AI infers" from contaminating each other. The fourth is the synthesis layer's "every claim must have an owner" and set-difference reporting. The fifth stands at the drafting layer, and it is the escalation point of the whole defense: **claim-level auditing** extends the object of inspection from "sentences bearing a citation" to "every substantive claim": descriptive claims must be anchored to a source; interpretive claims must state the author's position; causal claims must additionally pass the check of "does the strength of inference match the research design?" — failure on any one blocks finalization. Cross-sectional data written in causal language is stopped at exactly this gate. The sixth stands on the qualitative branch: **quotation-provenance auditing** requires the supporting excerpt of every code and theme to be anchored to "filename plus line number," each one re-read against the original transcript before delivery (an actual re-reading of the file, not box-ticking from conversational memory); any mismatch is deleted and an audit trail is left behind.

The design philosophy these six lines share is to trust no single point, no memory, and no self-verification: verification must change hands (be given to a fresh agent carrying no prior context), must return to the original material, and must leave an auditable trace.

### 3.4 Human-AI Responsibilities: The AI Guards the Gates, the Human Makes the Call

The human-machine division of labor across the pipeline follows one principle: **the AI is responsible for gatekeeping and production; the human retains every substantive decision**. Research direction, core question, and contribution claim are stated first by the user (commitment gate); the inclusion and exclusion of literature is adjudicated by the user (bucket confirmation); the promotion of finalized analyses into the literature library is executed manually by the user; every paragraph of the text is approved by the user, one by one. Conversely, the institution also guards against failure on the human side: confirmation mechanisms fatigue, so when several consecutive paragraphs receive nothing but "OK," the drafting stage detects it and prompts; approved content is protected by freezing — if the AI touches any frozen paragraph, it must show the before and after explicitly and obtain confirmation anew, preventing silent tampering during whole-passage regeneration. As for the AI's hard interceptions (such as paradigm mixing), a pass-through exit is preserved for the user's explicit insistence, with the ruling recorded and a risk flag attached before proceeding. To guard the gates without usurping the decision — that is the shared measure of restraint in every gate.

## 4. Governance and Version Discipline

A workflow that keeps being revised must itself be governed. The following five disciplines maintain the evolution of this system, and all of them generalize.

**A single authoritative original.** Each stage treats its source folder (main document, reference files, scripts) as the sole authoritative original; the packaged installer keeps only the current version; after an upgrade is repackaged, the old version is deleted outright, and version rollback relies on the original, the methodology log, and cloud version history. Keeping the original singular eliminates "multiple copies drifting apart."

**Repackage as a whole.** Upgrades always repackage and reinstall from the original in full; editing a single file inside the installed copy is forbidden. This rule comes from a real incident: updating the main document without synchronizing the reference files caused version drift between the main document and its sub-files, and the erratic behavior surfaced only weeks later. A companion drift-check script compares the installed copy against the original file by file, using hashes.

**An append-only methodology log.** Every upgrade appends one record to the corresponding methodology log (date, symptom, root cause, fix, verification method) — append only, never overwrite. The main document keeps only the current-version line, while the entire history of "why it came to be this way" migrates into the log, balancing traceability against loading cost.

**Stage, confirm, publish.** All modifications to control files and stages first enter a staging area and are published only after human confirmation: preview the diff first, back up automatically before overwriting, and always ask a human before deleting any file.

**Verification is never self-verification.** The agent that writes an output cannot serve as its own acceptance inspector. Once a rule or institutional document is written, a fresh agent carrying no prior context is dispatched to read it through and restate "what does this rule ask me to do." If the restatement drifts, the writing was vague. Code is actually run once, including error-input scenarios — reading the code is no substitute for executing it.

## 5. Design Principles and Pitfalls: What We Learned

This system was not designed in a single pass; it was iterated into being through repeated collisions, fixes, and verifications in real research projects. Below, organized by theme, are the lessons of the widest general value. If you intend to build or adapt a similar system, this section may be more useful than the architecture itself.

**Exception clauses are the breach in the rule.** I once banned AI inference across the board, allowing extrapolation only in the "application recommendations" section — and that section became the sole entry point through which fabrication slipped in. Lesson: every "inference permitted" outlet a process retains must be given its own dedicated audit. The later practice tags each application recommendation by its type of basis, with inference-type items force-flagged as pending human confirmation. By the same token, even the methodological tools an AI recommends can themselves be fabrications: an AI-produced deep-research report once recommended a third-party analysis tool that does not in fact exist — while the methodological framework it cited was real. Credibility comes in fragments; before building any new mechanism, verify its scholarly basis externally and adopt only the parts that can be traced.

**Environment failure and legitimately empty results must be distinguishable.** A retrieval API was once blocked by network permissions with a 403, and the script masked it as "ended normally, zero results"; the AI thereupon misjudged that "no literature exists under this keyword" and abandoned the entire direction. Lesson: error handling must never compress "the environment is broken" and "there is genuinely nothing" into one and the same signal; degradation to manual mode likewise needs an explicit output contract, with an explicit prohibition on padding the candidate pool with fabrications from training memory. In the same family of problems lies silent truncation: candidate papers were once hard-truncated to the top N by pre-ranking before semantic scoring, the remainder discarded unjudged and invisible to the user. The fix abolished silent truncation: on overflow, the stage halts and reports, returning the cost decision to the user.

**The most common death of an interface contract is unilateral definition.** An upgrade changed only the supplier side's output format while the consumer side never followed; the field definitions of one and the same table were scattered across three places, mutually inconsistent, and when things broke, the three parties each told a different story. Two lessons: the authoritative definition of a shared field may have only one home, with every other location pointing to it; and every interface change must be revised and accepted on both the supplying and consuming ends in the same batch.

**Examples are stronger than rules.** An old version number hard-coded into an output template was faithfully copied by the model, stamping every deliverable with the wrong seal. To a model, "copy the example" carries a higher probability than "follow the rule and change it"; the concrete strings inside an example are the strongest instruction of all. Fix: version numbers in templates are replaced with placeholders across the board, and the maintenance protocol explicitly bans hard-coding.

**Long-process degradation must be treated as an object of design.** Models forget early rules in long conversations, so each stage forcibly restates the most easily forgotten iron rules at the transition points between phases, batches, and paragraphs. But the restatement frequency must be operationalized as "once before each unit of work begins" — otherwise literal compliance turns into dozens of restatements per paragraph, diluting the warning instead. In the same family of mechanisms: approved paragraphs are written to file and frozen immediately; the review scoring plan is placed on record before the manuscript is read; correction responses undergo two-phase verification (the first pass is always marked "pending re-verification," and the re-verification input must be the manuscript as actually revised by the human — never an AI-produced annotated version verifying itself).

**State must land in files; it cannot live in the conversation.** A comprehensive red-team audit found that most anti-degradation mechanisms assumed "a single long conversation," while actual use advances intermittently across multiple conversations. Freeze lists, scoring baselines, gate states — if they exist only in conversational memory, they all evaporate the moment the conversation changes. The fix is a state-persistence contract unified across stages: "a state change means writing to file; startup means reading it back," with each stage specifying where its state files land and its detection procedure at startup. This lesson can be condensed into one sentence: **conversation is volatile memory; files are the hard disk.**

**Token cost is an engineering resource.** Batch analysis re-read a large template for every single paper; synthesis loaded every summary at once. At scale, either the cost explodes linearly or the model silently skims the later data (the latter is more dangerous, because it is silent). Fixes: read the template in full for the first paper, then re-read at intervals; beyond a threshold, split into batches, with intermediate products written to file; qualitative data proceeds session by session — "load, produce, write to file, release."

Looking back, the system's evolutionary arc runs roughly through five phases: first **division of labor and boundaries** (cutting a single grand process into seven stages, disambiguating trigger boundaries pair by pair), then **architectural layering** (main documents keep the decisions, templates delegated down into reference files), then building the **authenticity defense line** (citation-level checking upgraded to claim-level auditing and material-first), then treating **the human-AI collaboration loop's own tendency to break** as an object of design (freezing, concession thresholds, confirmation-fatigue detection), and finally the red-team-audit-driven remediation **from rules to carriers** (state persistence, two-sided interface contracts, making environment failure explicit). Running through it all is institutionalized maintenance: a single authoritative original, whole-package repacking, hash verification, append-only logs — making every improvement traceable, verifiable, and replicable.

## 6. Genealogy and Licensing

The genealogy of this workflow deserves an honest accounting. The first six of the seven stages (research design, literature search, per-paper analysis, cross-paper synthesis, chapter drafting, review diagnosis) are the author's original construction, iterated into shape within my own research projects first; the open-source package on GitHub, [Imbad0202/academic-research-skills](https://github.com/Imbad0202/academic-research-skills) (hereafter ARS), is an **external reference system** introduced at a later stage. In three waves I compared my self-built system against it item by item, borrowing its mechanism-design ideas to calibrate my own weaknesses, but never copying its document content. The three waves of comparison adopted mechanisms such as design-phase adversarial checking, the commitment gate, the reviewer self-bias checklist, and the material-first clause; they also rejected a good number: rejecting the calibration gold-standard set whose maintenance cost is too high for a single person (over-engineering), and rejecting an audit package claiming to detect plagiarism (manufacturing a false sense of security is worse than having none). The criterion for adoption remained constant throughout: aimed at the real weaknesses of one's own environment, low in engineering and high in leverage, and not duplicating existing mechanisms.

Of the seven, only the seventh (qualitative thematic analysis) was produced by adapting the corresponding ARS content, and is therefore released under its original license, CC BY-NC 4.0, with thanks to the original author; its "one skill, two paths" front-end design is this repository's own structure. The other six stages, together with this white paper and the other documents, are under CC BY 4.0, and the code in each stage's scripts/ directory is separately under the MIT license. The three-part licensing is detailed in the repository's LICENSE.md. The positioning sums up in one sentence: **an original methodology, plus calibration against an external reference system**.

## 7. The On-Ramp, and Honest Boundaries

### 7.1 Start Here

The repository provides a complete starter kit: `starter-kit/` contains the 00-05 folder skeleton and templates for the four control files (each with filling-in guidance and fictional example sentences), and `skills/` contains the full source files of the seven stages together with their packaged installers. The minimal on-ramp is three steps. Step one: clone the repository and prepare the control files — all four templates must remain in `00_專案控制/` (the skills read them directly; a missing file will break the stage), but **on the first run you need to hand-customize only two**: `about-me.md` and `research-identity.md` (`writing-standards.md` keeps its default provisions, and `portfolio-status.md` may be left empty). These two can be neither skipped nor done perfunctorily — the quality of the control files directly determines the quality of AI collaboration, and the "self-declaration of writing habits" in particular demands honesty with yourself. Step two: install the seven stages (or begin with just the two — research design and per-paper analysis). Step three: run one real, small topic through the minimal chain from research design to per-paper analysis, get a feel for the rhythm of stage hand-offs and the human-AI division of labor, then expand step by step to the full pipeline. Step-by-step operating instructions are in the repository's [quick-start guide](./quick-start-guide.md).

**A note on environments.** The pipeline form described in this paper — especially §2.4's "`05_輸出/` is the sole write zone" and §5's "state must land in files" — presupposes a persistent workspace folder, that is, Cowork mode in the Claude desktop app, or Claude Code. But this is not the only entrance to the seven stages: they can equally be installed on the claude.ai web version (Skills are available from the Free plan upward), in which case the stages degrade into seven standalone tools, hand-offs between stages and state continuation are accomplished by the user manually moving files, and `literature-search`'s automated search is unavailable owing to the sandbox domain whitelist. The institution itself is unchanged; only the executor switches from skill to human. See [using-on-claude-ai.md](./using-on-claude-ai.md) for details.

### 7.2 What This Institution Cannot Fix

Finally, the honesty clause. Decomposition, verification, and multi-layer auditing can fix **execution quality**, but two classes of problems lie beyond what institution can fix, and the workflow's institutional files declare this about themselves in writing. The first is **ill-defined problems**: when the requirement itself has not yet taken shape ("make this paper more insightful"), the AI has no standard answer to execute; the correct move is to convert it into concrete, acceptance-testable requirements, or to offer directional samples for the user to choose among — not to feign confidence. The second is **judgments of taste**: how deep the theoretical dialogue should run, the fine calibration of a reviewer's tone — the ceiling for such judgments is the level of template plus checklist, and what institution can do is align strictly with existing exemplars and state plainly that "this is a judgment of taste; reviewing it personally is recommended."

The boundaries of applicability call for the same honesty: this workflow's literature-search process has transparent counts and logs, but it is **not** a systematic review tool and must not be claimed as one in a paper; the citation-authenticity defense line greatly reduces the risk of fabrication, but cannot replace the author's final responsibility for every single citation; the qualitative branch's de-identification gate is a technical safeguard, not a substitute for research-ethics review.

And every ethical line, in the end, condenses into a single criterion: **are you capable of taking responsibility for the AI's output?** Using AI to accelerate work you already command — you have read the literature and have it organize the summaries; you understand the statistical methods and have it run the analysis — is an efficiency tool. Using AI to generate content you do not understand and cannot check for correctness is an integrity problem. This line has nothing to do with the tool and everything to do with the user. It is also the real reason behind the current academic consensus that "AI must not be listed as an author": authorship means bearing responsibility, and AI cannot bear it. When something goes wrong, the name left on the retraction record is yours.

AI is an extraordinarily capable research assistant, and this institution makes it trustworthy to the point of "safe to delegate to" — but the paper is forever your paper, and the judgment forever your judgment. A line from Andrej Karpathy may stand as the footnote to this entire piece: "You can outsource your thinking, but you can't outsource your understanding." This workflow is, if anything, more conservative still: it does not let you outsource even your thinking wholesale — the thinking layer requires you to set pen to paper first. What it truly takes into custody is execution alone.
